The Two-Faced Deity

Who is Janus? While other Roman gods had Greek counterparts (Jupiter was Zeus, Mars was Ares), Janus was uniquely Roman. He existed before the others, present at the separation of the elements during the creation of the cosmos.

He is the "Janitor" of the Universeâ€”the keeper of the celestial gates. No prayer could reach the other gods without first passing through Janus.

His most famous attribute is his duality. He is Bifrons (Two-Faced): one face looks to the past (History/Regret), and the other looks to the future (Hope/Destiny). This makes him the perfect patron for the first month of the year.

Why "January" in English?

The journey from Ancient Rome to Modern English involves a linguistic evolution and a history of the alphabet itself.

IANUA Latin for "Door" or "Arcade"
➞
IANUARIUS "Month of Janus"
(Added by King Numa Pompilius, 713 BC)
➞
JANUARY Modern English
Wait, why "J" instead of "I"? In Classical Latin, the letter J did not exist. The Romans wrote Ianus. The letter "I" functioned as both a vowel and a consonant (pronounced like 'Y' in 'Year'). The letter "J" was only distinguished from "I" in the Middle Ages to represent the consonantal sound. Thus, Ianuarius became January.

Symbols of Authority

🗝

The Golden Key

Janus holds the key in his left hand. He alone has the power to open the new year, lock away the old year, and open the gates of heaven for the sun to rise.

⚔

The Staff

In his right hand, he wields a staff (virga) to drive away intruders and guide travelers safely through the threshold of time.

🏛

The Gates of War

The Ianua Geminus temple gates were open during war and closed during peace. Rarely were they ever closed, signifying Rome's constant battles.
